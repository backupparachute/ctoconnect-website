Every standup sounds productive. People are busy. Tickets are moving. Branches are getting merged. But when you zoom out and ask what actually shipped this month, the room gets quiet.

That's motion. It feels like progress. It looks like progress in every dashboard and status report. But it's not.

I've watched teams spend entire quarters refactoring code that was working fine. Migrating to a new framework because the old one felt dated. Reorganizing their project structure for the third time. Every one of those tasks generated activity. None of them moved the product forward.

The worst part is that motion is self-reinforcing. Process creates more process. A new planning meeting leads to a new review meeting leads to a new sync meeting. Everyone's calendar is full. Everyone feels busy. Nobody's asking whether the work matters.

## How to Tell the Difference

Progress changes something for the user or the business. A feature ships. A bug that's been hurting customers gets fixed. Performance improves in a way users notice. Revenue goes up. Churn goes down.

Motion is everything else. It might be necessary. Refactors sometimes are. Meetings sometimes are. But if you can't draw a line from the work to an outcome, you should be asking hard questions about whether it needs to happen right now.

## What to Do About It

Start asking one question at every standup and every sprint review: what did we ship? Not what did we work on. Not what's in progress. What's done, deployed, and in front of users?

Teams that measure motion will optimize for motion. Teams that measure shipped outcomes will optimize for shipped outcomes. Pick the right metric.
